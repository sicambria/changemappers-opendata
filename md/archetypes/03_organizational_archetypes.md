# Organizational Archetypes

These archetypes describe how organizations actually function internally—their decision-making structures, power dynamics, culture, and accountability mechanisms. They are distinct from community archetypes (how people live together) and network archetypes (how movements operate). Organizational archetypes determine *how* changemaker energy and network strategies actually get implemented in practice.

---

## 1. Hierarchical Command-and-Control
**Description:** Decisions flow top-down. Authority is concentrated at the top. Accountability runs upward.
**Strengths:** Speed of decision-making, clarity of accountability, ability to execute at scale.
**Shadow:** Authoritarianism, siloed knowledge, suppression of innovation from below, single point of failure.
**Common In:** Large NGOs, government agencies, traditional nonprofits.

---

## 2. Consensus-Based / Participatory Democracy
**Description:** Everyone has voice in decisions. High emphasis on inclusion and shared ownership.
**Strengths:** Deep buy-in, collective intelligence, equitable power distribution.
**Shadow:** Paralysis, endless meetings, tyranny of the minority blocking progress, decision fatigue.
**Common In:** Activist collectives, cooperative organizations, some intentional communities.

---

## 3. Distributed / Network-Based
**Description:** Autonomous nodes coordinate loosely around shared principles. No central authority.
**Strengths:** Resilience, adaptability, scale without bureaucracy.
**Shadow:** Incoherence, duplicated effort, diffusion of accountability, difficulty coordinating on complex tasks.
**Common In:** Open source movements, decentralized networks, Catalyst 2030 style organizations.

---

## 4. Meritocratic
**Description:** Influence flows to whoever has the most expertise, track record, or demonstrated impact.
**Strengths:** Quality of decisions, reward for excellence, knowledge-driven culture.
**Shadow:** Elitism, gatekeeping of knowledge, exclusion of community wisdom, reinforcing existing hierarchies.
**Common In:** Academic institutions, think tanks, technical organizations.

---

## 5. Rotating Leadership / Radical Transparency
**Description:** Leadership rotates among members. Information is completely open. Power is intentionally distributed.
**Strengths:** Prevention of power concentration, shared learning, organizational resilience.
**Shadow:** Instability, burnout of people constantly cycling through leadership, loss of institutional knowledge.
**Common In:** Some cooperatives, radical democratic organizations, some indigenous governance models.

---

## 6. Purpose-Driven / Mission-Aligned
**Description:** The shared purpose becomes the primary organizing principle rather than structure or hierarchy. The mission is the boss.
**Strengths:** Alignment, motivation, coherence across distributed teams.
**Shadow:** Cultishness, mission-creep justifying anything, difficulty challenging leadership when purpose is conflated with individuals.
**Common In:** Social enterprises, impact organizations, some religious or spiritual organizations.

---

## 7. Stewardship / Trustee-Based
**Description:** Leaders see themselves as temporary caretakers of the organization's mission, not owners. Emphasis on intergenerational responsibility.
**Strengths:** Long-term thinking, humility in leadership, mission preservation over personal legacy.
**Shadow:** Abdication of responsibility, lack of decisive leadership, difficulty adapting when stewardship becomes passivity.
**Common In:** Foundations, endowment-based organizations, some indigenous governance structures.

---

## Relationship to Other Archetype Systems

- The same **change network archetype** can be implemented through very different organizational archetypes with very different results.
- For example: a **direct-action network** run as hierarchical command-and-control looks very different from one run as a distributed network.
- **Shadow archetypes** often emerge from organizational archetype mismatches—when a purpose-driven mission is housed in a hierarchical structure, the shadow is authoritarian mission-capture.
- **Changemappers** can help changemakers identify not just *what* they want to work on, but *what kind of organizational culture* they'll thrive in.
