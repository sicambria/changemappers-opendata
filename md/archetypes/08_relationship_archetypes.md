# Relationship Archetypes

These archetypes describe how the twelve change network archetypes actually relate to each other—complementary, competitive, antagonistic, or hierarchical. Understanding relationship archetypes is essential for coordination, coalition-building, and avoiding wasted conflict between movements that could be allies.

---

## 1. Complementary / Symbiotic
**Description:** Two archetypes naturally strengthen each other. Each does what the other cannot.
**Examples:**
- Direct action creates urgency → Policy advocacy provides solutions when the window opens
- Presencing builds inner capacity → Catalyst 2030 connects the transformed leaders
- Regenerative agriculture rebuilds local systems → Circular economy scales those principles
**Key Insight:** Complementary archetypes need each other but often don't recognize it. The relationship requires intentional bridging.

---

## 2. Sequential / Pipeline
**Description:** One archetype creates the conditions for the next to operate. They work in succession rather than simultaneously.
**Examples:**
- Direct action disrupts → Justice frameworks name what's needed → Policy advocates codify it → Impact investors fund it → B-corporations scale it
- Grassroots organizing builds power → Transition towns build alternatives → Policy advocacy locks them in
**Key Insight:** Pipeline relationships fail when one link breaks or moves too fast for the others. Coordination of timing is critical.

---

## 3. Competitive / Siloed
**Description:** Two archetypes pursue similar goals through different means and compete for resources, attention, or legitimacy without coordinating.
**Examples:**
- Policy advocacy vs. direct action (competing for narrative control)
- Impact investing vs. gift economy (competing theories of how change gets funded)
- Grassroots organizing vs. think tanks (competing claims to expertise)
**Key Insight:** Competition is often unnecessary and is fueled by scarcity mindsets and poor communication. Changemappers can reveal complementarity beneath apparent competition.

---

## 4. Antagonistic / In Tension
**Description:** Two archetypes have genuinely different values or theories of change that create real tension, not just communication failure.
**Examples:**
- B-corporations vs. just transition (market-based change vs. worker power-based change)
- Circular economy vs. degrowth (efficiency within growth vs. questioning growth itself)
- Scientific knowledge vs. indigenous knowledge (different epistemologies that resist easy synthesis)
**Key Insight:** Antagonistic relationships should not be forced into false harmony. The tension can be generative if held consciously and with respect.

---

## 5. Hierarchical / Enabling
**Description:** One archetype creates the enabling conditions—resources, legitimacy, infrastructure—within which another can operate.
**Examples:**
- Impact investing enables social enterprise and B-corporations
- Policy advocacy enables regulatory frameworks within which circular economy can scale
- Presencing enables the inner capacity that makes all other archetypes more effective
**Key Insight:** Enabling archetypes often don't get credit for what they make possible. Making these relationships visible prevents resentment and builds appreciation.

---

## 6. Parallel / Independent
**Description:** Two archetypes operate in the same space without significant interaction—neither complementary nor competitive.
**Examples:**
- Transition Towns and impact investing (different scales and mechanisms, minimal overlap)
- Indigenous knowledge systems and think tanks (operating in different epistemic worlds)
**Key Insight:** Parallel relationships represent missed opportunities. Changemappers can reveal unexpected connections and catalyze new collaboration.

---

## 7. Generative / Emergent
**Description:** Two or more archetypes in combination produce something that neither could produce alone—genuine emergence.
**Examples:**
- Presencing + direct action + climate justice = transformative movements with both inner depth and outer power
- Circular economy + indigenous knowledge + bioregional scale = genuinely regenerative economic systems
- Just transition + impact investing + community organizing = worker-owned green economy
**Key Insight:** Generative combinations are the highest-value relationships in the ecosystem. They are rare and require intentional design. Changemappers exists partly to catalyze these combinations.

## Notes on Using This Framework

- Relationship archetypes shift over time and context. Two antagonistic archetypes on one issue may be complementary on another.
- The most important insight: most competitive relationships between change movements are unnecessary and emerge from scarcity, poor communication, or failure to understand each other's theory of change.
- Changemappers' core function is to make these relationship archetypes visible and navigable—helping changemakers find their natural allies and manage their genuine tensions consciously.
