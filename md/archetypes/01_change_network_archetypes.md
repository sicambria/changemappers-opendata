# Change Network Archetypes

These twelve archetypes describe the different types of movements and organizations working toward humanity-scale change. Each has a distinct theory of change and leverage point.

---

## 1. Inner Consciousness Shifting
**Example:** Presencing Institute (MIT Theory U)
**Theory of Change:** Shift how leaders think and perceive, and outer systems will follow.
**Leverage Point:** Awareness and inner development as the foundation for outer transformation.
**Shadow:** Can become navel-gazing or disconnected from urgent material realities.

---

## 2. Network Coordination of Social Innovators
**Example:** Catalyst 2030
**Theory of Change:** Connect and amplify existing social innovators into a coherent global movement.
**Leverage Point:** Emergent systemic change through coordination and collective action.
**Shadow:** Can become a loose network without deep transformation or accountability.

---

## 3. Direct Action and Disruption
**Example:** Extinction Rebellion, Earth First!
**Theory of Change:** Disruption and visibility force institutions to respond.
**Leverage Point:** Public attention, shock, and civil disobedience opening political space.
**Shadow:** Burnout, alienation of potential allies, crisis addiction.

---

## 4. Climate and Environmental Justice
**Example:** Climate Justice Alliance, frontline community organizations
**Theory of Change:** Centering marginalized and frontline communities' power reframes solutions around equity.
**Leverage Point:** Shifting who has power in defining and implementing solutions.
**Shadow:** Can fragment coalitions or become siloed from broader movements.

---

## 5. Regenerative Agriculture and Permaculture
**Example:** Rodale Institute, permaculture networks
**Theory of Change:** Rebuild soil, food systems, and ecological relationships from the ground up.
**Leverage Point:** Local food sovereignty and regenerative land practices.
**Shadow:** Can remain small-scale and disconnected from systemic policy change.

---

## 6. Circular Economy and Zero-Waste
**Example:** Ellen MacArthur Foundation
**Theory of Change:** Redesign production systems to eliminate waste and close resource loops.
**Leverage Point:** Industrial and supply chain transformation.
**Shadow:** Can be co-opted by greenwashing without addressing root causes.

---

## 7. Transition Towns and Community Resilience
**Example:** Transition Network
**Theory of Change:** Local resilience and community bonds are the foundation of systemic change.
**Leverage Point:** Hyperlocal interdependence and place-based organizing.
**Shadow:** Can remain local without scaling to systemic policy or economic change.

---

## 8. Policy Advocacy and Think Tanks
**Example:** Rocky Mountain Institute, policy NGOs
**Theory of Change:** Changing laws and regulations drives systemic change at scale.
**Leverage Point:** Government policy, regulation, and institutional reform.
**Shadow:** Can become disconnected from grassroots reality or captured by political cycles.

---

## 9. Impact Investing and Social Finance
**Example:** RSF Social Finance, B Lab, Omidyar Network
**Theory of Change:** Redirecting capital flows toward missions creates systemic market pressure.
**Leverage Point:** Capital allocation and financial system redesign.
**Shadow:** Can prioritize financial returns over genuine impact; market logic can distort mission.

---

## 10. B-Corporations and Benefit Companies
**Example:** Patagonia, B Lab certified companies
**Theory of Change:** Embedding purpose into business DNA proves that profit and mission can align.
**Leverage Point:** Business structure and market incentives.
**Shadow:** Can remain marginal within dominant extractive market systems.

---

## 11. Just Transition
**Example:** Movement Generation, Climate Justice Alliance
**Theory of Change:** Pair worker power and labor organizing with environmental goals.
**Leverage Point:** Coalition between labor unions and frontline environmental communities.
**Shadow:** Tension between immediate worker interests and long-term ecological goals.

---

## 12. Grassroots Community Organizing
**Example:** Local environmental justice organizations
**Theory of Change:** Hyperlocal place-based power is the foundation of lasting change.
**Leverage Point:** Community ownership, local knowledge, and place attachment.
**Shadow:** Can struggle to scale or connect to systemic change levers.

---

## Synergistic Combinations

- **Direct action** creates urgency and opens political space → **Policy advocates** rush in with solutions
- **Regenerative agriculture** builds local resilience → **Circular economy** scales those principles
- **Climate justice** centers equity → **Impact investing** funds it
- **Presencing** builds inner capacity for leaders → **Catalyst 2030** connects the players
- **Just transition** protects workers → **B-corporations** prove the business case
- **Grassroots organizing** holds local power → prevents top-down solutions dominating

The twelve archetypes are different instruments. The meta-question is: who conducts the orchestra?
