# Resource Archetypes

These archetypes describe how different movements and organizations fuel themselves—their resource models, funding strategies, and economic logic. Resource archetypes profoundly shape what an organization can do, how independent it is, and what constraints it operates under.

---

## 1. Volunteer and Gift Economy
**Description:** Work is sustained by donated time, skills, and resources. No formal financial transactions.
**Strengths:** High intrinsic motivation, independence from funders, authentic community building.
**Shadow:** Burnout, unsustainability, exclusion of people who can't afford to work for free.
**Examples:** Many grassroots movements, open source communities, some mutual aid networks.

---

## 2. Philanthropic and Donor-Funded
**Description:** Sustained by grants and donations from foundations, individual donors, or government agencies.
**Strengths:** Ability to fund high-risk or non-commercial work, long-term mission focus.
**Shadow:** Mission drift toward funder priorities, dependency, organizational precarity, power imbalance with funders.
**Examples:** Most NGOs, nonprofits, policy think tanks.

---

## 3. Impact Investing and Social Finance
**Description:** Funded through investment capital that expects both social return and some financial return.
**Strengths:** Scale, sustainability, market discipline, alignment of financial and social incentives.
**Shadow:** Financial returns can distort mission; market logic may exclude the most marginalized.
**Examples:** Social enterprises, community development finance institutions, some cooperative networks.

---

## 4. Earned Revenue and Social Enterprise
**Description:** Generate income through selling products or services whose delivery is itself the mission.
**Strengths:** Financial independence, market feedback, scalability.
**Shadow:** Commercial pressures can erode mission; risk of becoming indistinguishable from conventional business.
**Examples:** Fair trade enterprises, community land trusts, cooperative businesses.

---

## 5. Commons and Shared Ownership
**Description:** Resources are collectively owned and managed by a community. Value flows back to members rather than extracted by owners.
**Strengths:** Equity, sustainability, alignment of incentives with community wellbeing.
**Shadow:** Governance complexity, difficulty scaling, vulnerability to enclosure by market or state actors.
**Examples:** Cooperatives, community land trusts, open source projects, some indigenous resource management.

---

## 6. Government and Public Funding
**Description:** Sustained by public money through contracts, grants, or direct provision.
**Strengths:** Scale, legitimacy, stability, universal access potential.
**Shadow:** Political vulnerability, bureaucratic constraints, dependency on electoral cycles, mission compromise for compliance.
**Examples:** Public health systems, government-funded research, some community organizations.

---

## 7. Decentralized and Crypto-Enabled
**Description:** Sustained through decentralized mechanisms—DAOs, tokenization, peer-to-peer exchange, or timebanking.
**Strengths:** Independence from traditional financial institutions, transparency, potential for new equity models.
**Shadow:** Volatility, speculation, exclusion of people without technical access, governance immaturity.
**Examples:** Some open source movements, experimental cooperative platforms, mutual credit systems.

---

## Notes on Using This Framework

- Resource archetypes often determine the real constraints on a changemaker's work more than anything else.
- Misalignment between resource archetype and network archetype creates persistent tension—for example, a direct-action movement funded by philanthropic foundations faces constant pressure to moderate its tactics.
- Changemappers can help people understand which resource models are available to their archetype, and connect them with others who have navigated similar funding challenges.
- The most resilient movements often combine multiple resource archetypes rather than depending on a single source.
