# Scale Archetypes

These archetypes describe the geographic and social scale at which change work primarily operates. Scale archetypes have their own logics, leverage points, and constraints. Misunderstanding scale—trying to apply hyperlocal logic globally, or global logic locally—is one of the most common sources of failure in change work.

---

## 1. Household and Personal Scale
**Description:** Change begins with individual behavior, household practices, and personal transformation.
**Leverage Point:** Daily choices, personal relationships, immediate influence sphere.
**Strengths:** Authenticity, embodied change, immediate feedback.
**Shadow:** Can become lifestyle activism disconnected from systemic change; individualism as a substitute for collective action.
**Examples:** Permaculture homesteads, personal sustainability practices, inner development work.

---

## 2. Neighborhood and Hyperlocal
**Description:** Work is rooted in a specific place—a block, a neighborhood, a village.
**Leverage Point:** Place attachment, local trust, community ownership.
**Strengths:** Deep accountability, cultural specificity, genuine community buy-in.
**Shadow:** NIMBY-ism, difficulty seeing beyond local interests, can't address problems that originate elsewhere.
**Examples:** Neighborhood organizations, local food systems, community gardens, mutual aid networks.

---

## 3. City and Municipal
**Description:** Work operates at the level of a city or municipality—often the most dynamic scale for policy innovation.
**Leverage Point:** Local government, urban density, proximity of diverse stakeholders.
**Strengths:** Enough scale to matter, close enough to implement, cities often lead where nations follow.
**Shadow:** City boundaries don't match ecological or social systems; gentrification and displacement risks.
**Examples:** C40 Cities, urban transition towns, city-level climate plans.

---

## 4. Bioregional
**Description:** Work is organized around ecological regions—watersheds, forest systems, food sheds—rather than political boundaries.
**Leverage Point:** Ecological coherence, natural system boundaries.
**Strengths:** Aligns human systems with ecological reality, enables genuine regeneration.
**Shadow:** Doesn't map to existing governance structures, difficult to fund and organize politically.
**Examples:** Watershed councils, bioregional food systems, some indigenous governance models.

---

## 5. National
**Description:** Work operates at the level of the nation-state—legislation, national culture, federal policy.
**Leverage Point:** Legal frameworks, national culture, electoral systems, federal budgets.
**Strengths:** Enormous leverage when policy changes, clear accountability structures.
**Shadow:** Slow, vulnerable to electoral cycles, can erase local and regional diversity.
**Examples:** National policy advocacy organizations, national social movements.

---

## 6. Regional and Transnational
**Description:** Work spans multiple nations within a region—the EU, Latin America, Sub-Saharan Africa.
**Leverage Point:** Regional institutions, shared culture and ecology across borders.
**Strengths:** Can coordinate across nations while remaining contextually relevant.
**Shadow:** Complex governance, unequal power between nations, difficult to hold accountability.
**Examples:** EU climate policy, regional development banks, transnational justice movements.

---

## 7. Global
**Description:** Work operates at planetary scale—global institutions, international agreements, global culture.
**Leverage Point:** International frameworks, global capital flows, planetary consciousness.
**Strengths:** Only scale at which some problems (climate, biodiversity, inequality) can be addressed.
**Shadow:** Remote from lived reality, captured by powerful interests, difficult to implement locally.
**Examples:** UN SDG framework, Catalyst 2030, international climate agreements.

---

## Notes on Using This Framework

- The most effective change strategies work across multiple scales simultaneously—rooted locally, networked regionally, aligned globally.
- Scale archetypes reveal why movements talk past each other: a bioregional thinker and a national policy advocate may share goals but operate in completely different logics.
- Changemappers can help people find allies operating at complementary scales and build multi-scale coalitions.
- The question is not which scale is most important, but how change at one scale enables and reinforces change at others.
