# Failure Archetypes

These archetypes describe the characteristic ways that change movements collapse, get co-opted, stagnate, or burn out. Understanding failure modes explicitly is one of the most powerful tools for preventing them. These are not the same as shadow archetypes—shadows are distortions of individual strengths, while failure archetypes are systemic breakdown patterns.

---

## 1. Co-optation
**Description:** The movement's language, symbols, or goals are adopted by the very systems it sought to change, while the transformative substance is stripped away.
**How it happens:** Success attracts mainstream attention; mainstream actors adopt the frame without the content; the movement becomes a brand rather than a force.
**Warning Signs:** Corporate partnerships without accountability, movement leaders hired into institutions, metrics replace mission.
**Examples:** Sustainability becoming greenwashing, diversity and inclusion becoming corporate PR.
**Prevention:** Maintain clear non-negotiables, keep power with frontline communities, resist premature mainstreaming.

---

## 2. Fragmentation
**Description:** The movement splinters into competing factions, losing collective power through internal conflict.
**How it happens:** Ideological purity tests, competition for funding, personality conflicts, unresolved power dynamics, lack of shared governance.
**Warning Signs:** More energy spent on internal conflict than external change, public infighting, multiplication of splinter organizations.
**Examples:** Historical splits in labor movements, left political fragmentation.
**Prevention:** Clear governance, shared purpose over ideology, conflict resolution infrastructure, abundance rather than scarcity mindset.

---

## 3. Burnout and Depletion
**Description:** The people sustaining the movement exhaust themselves and can no longer continue.
**How it happens:** Insufficient resources, martyrdom culture, no boundaries between work and life, no renewal practices, crisis perpetuation.
**Warning Signs:** High turnover, decreasing volunteer engagement, leaders leaving, cynicism spreading.
**Examples:** Activist burnout, nonprofit sector exhaustion, social entrepreneur depletion.
**Prevention:** Regenerative culture, contributor protection, rest as a structural feature not a reward, Changemappers-style mutual support.

---

## 4. Mission Drift
**Description:** The organization gradually shifts its activities away from its original purpose, usually toward what is fundable or popular.
**How it happens:** Funder pressure, leadership change, success in one area attracting resources away from core work, scaling without alignment.
**Warning Signs:** Original beneficiaries no longer central, metrics disconnect from mission, founding members leave frustrated.
**Examples:** Nonprofits that become primarily grant-writing machines, advocacy organizations that become think tanks.
**Prevention:** Mission lock structures, community accountability, regular purpose audits, stewardship governance.

---

## 5. Premature Scaling
**Description:** A successful local or small-scale model is scaled too quickly, losing the contextual intelligence that made it work.
**How it happens:** Funder enthusiasm, competitive pressure, replication without adaptation, systems that worked intimately failing at distance.
**Warning Signs:** Loss of quality at scale, community alienation, leadership becoming remote from implementation.
**Examples:** Many international development programs, some social franchise models.
**Prevention:** Slow intentional scaling, local adaptation, preserving relational infrastructure, distributed rather than centralized scaling.

---

## 6. Institutionalization and Calcification
**Description:** A once-vital movement becomes an institution that primarily serves its own perpetuation rather than its mission.
**How it happens:** Success breeds structure; structure breeds bureaucracy; bureaucracy breeds risk-aversion; risk-aversion kills transformation.
**Warning Signs:** Innovation moves to the margins, leadership becomes more concerned with organizational survival than impact, founding energy is lost.
**Examples:** Many large environmental NGOs, established unions, some foundations.
**Prevention:** Built-in renewal mechanisms, sunset clauses, regular strategy reinvention, welcoming disruption from within.

---

## 7. Repression and Backlash
**Description:** The movement is actively suppressed by powerful interests threatened by its success.
**How it happens:** Success; direct threat to entrenched power; criminalization, surveillance, smear campaigns, legal harassment.
**Warning Signs:** Legal challenges, media attacks, infiltration, funding withdrawn by pressure, leaders targeted.
**Examples:** Civil rights movement repression, environmental activist criminalization, labor union suppression.
**Prevention:** Legal infrastructure, decentralization of power and knowledge, international solidarity networks, documentation and transparency.

---

## 8. Success Without Transformation
**Description:** The movement achieves its stated goals but the underlying system remains unchanged or adapts to absorb the win.
**How it happens:** Narrow goal-setting, treating symptoms rather than causes, winning battles while losing the war.
**Warning Signs:** Celebrating wins while conditions worsen, inability to articulate next goals, system regenerates the problem in new form.
**Examples:** Winning specific environmental regulations while industrial system expands; poverty programs that don't address wealth concentration.
**Prevention:** Systems-level goal-setting, theory of change that names root causes, celebrating wins without declaring victory.

---

## Notes on Using This Framework

- Every movement is vulnerable to multiple failure archetypes simultaneously.
- The most important question is not whether failure will come, but whether the movement has the awareness and infrastructure to recognize and recover from it.
- Changemappers can serve as early warning infrastructure—connecting changemakers who recognize failure patterns with others who have navigated them.
- Understanding failure archetypes is not pessimism—it is the most practical form of strategic resilience.
