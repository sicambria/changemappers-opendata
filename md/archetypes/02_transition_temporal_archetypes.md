# Transition-Temporal Archetypes

Transition and time are inseparable. These archetypes describe both the journey a changemaker takes and the timescale at which their work unfolds. Each archetype captures a distinct rhythm of change, a transition pathway, and a characteristic shadow.

---

## 1. Emergency-to-Organizational
**Timescale:** Immediate → 1–5 years
**Description:** Work begins in crisis response—urgent, reactive, high-intensity. Over time it transitions into sustained institutional structures.
**Theory of Change:** Act now or it's too late. Then build what endures.
**Shadow:** Losing urgency and becoming bureaucratic; or never leaving crisis mode and burning out.
**Example Movements:** Disaster relief organizations, emergency climate campaigns.

---

## 2. Electoral-to-Generational
**Timescale:** 2–4 year cycles → 20–30 years
**Description:** Work begins within electoral and campaign windows, then transitions toward long-term systemic and cultural change.
**Theory of Change:** Win policy windows now while planting seeds for generational transformation.
**Shadow:** Selling out principles for electoral viability; or losing political relevance while pursuing slow change.
**Example Movements:** Climate policy advocacy, progressive political organizing.

---

## 3. Organizational-to-Evolutionary
**Timescale:** 5–10 years → 50–100+ years
**Description:** Work begins as a social enterprise or organization, then transitions toward civilizational-scale regenerative change across decades.
**Theory of Change:** Build institutions that evolve beyond their founders into enduring systems.
**Shadow:** Diluting impact through overscaling; or losing focus and becoming irrelevant.
**Example Movements:** Regenerative agriculture movements, long-term systems change organizations.

---

## 4. Cyclical-to-Perpetual
**Timescale:** Seasonal/natural rhythms → beyond linear time
**Description:** Work follows natural cycles—seasonal, lunar, ecological—and transitions into understanding itself as part of eternal, non-linear systems.
**Theory of Change:** Align with natural rhythms; balance rather than winning is the goal.
**Shadow:** Spiritual bypassing or inaction; being trapped in repetition without progressing.
**Example Movements:** Permaculture, indigenous stewardship practices, regenerative land movements.

---

## 5. Grassroots-to-Systemic
**Timescale:** Immediate local → 10–30 years systemic
**Description:** Work begins hyperlocally and transitions toward influencing regional or national systems.
**Theory of Change:** Local roots create the foundation for systemic change that actually sticks.
**Shadow:** Getting stuck at local scale; or losing local accountability when scaling.
**Example Movements:** Transition Towns, community organizing networks.

---

## 6. Disruptive-to-Constructive
**Timescale:** Short-term disruption → medium-term construction
**Description:** Work begins by breaking open the status quo, then transitions into building new systems and structures.
**Theory of Change:** You must clear the old before the new can emerge.
**Shadow:** Remaining in permanent disruption without building alternatives; or losing momentum when shifting to constructive work.
**Example Movements:** Direct action movements transitioning into policy or cooperative development.

---

## Notes on Using This Framework

- Most changemakers begin in one temporal archetype and need to consciously navigate transitions.
- The shadow of each archetype often emerges precisely at the transition point.
- Changemappers can help people identify where they are in their transition journey and connect them with others who have navigated similar transitions.
- Awareness of timescale prevents misalignment—emergency responders and evolutionary thinkers often frustrate each other without realizing they're operating in different temporal frames.
